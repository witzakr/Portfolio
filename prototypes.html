Our group worked on two prototypes for our project: one for mobile devices and another for laptops. The mobile prototype was designed to meet our client's request for a responsive website on mobile devices, while the laptop prototype served as the base for stationary devices. Despite having the same functions, the mobile version was a scaled-down version of the laptop prototype.
The main differences between the two prototypes include the display for artwork and the navigation layout. In the mobile version, the navigation is placed in a dropdown menu instead of a bar on top of the website, and the buttons are larger to enhance usability on smaller screens.
Creating a prototype can take time, but I find it enjoyable. It's a culmination of all the phases we've gone through and the lessons we've learned. One important thing to keep in mind is not to overdo it. Since it's just a prototype, there's no need to include excessive details. A basic layout for the website and some functional elements, like navigation, suffice. I've learned this the hard way in the past, as I used to include too many details or plan prototypes incorrectly. This made the development process for other projects more challenging. However, the experience gained from those situations has been valuable.
